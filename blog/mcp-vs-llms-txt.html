MCP vs. llms.txt: Which AI Optimization Standard Does Your Site Actually Need?

Two standards are reshaping how AI agents interact with websites. They solve different problems — and the choice of which to implement first depends entirely on what your site needs AI agents to do.

The Question Everyone Is Asking Wrong

Search "MCP vs llms.txt" and you'll find a lot of takes that frame this as a competition — as if you need to pick a side. That framing is wrong, and it leads teams to skip the standard that would actually help them most.

MCP (Model Context Protocol) is a protocol for giving AI agents the ability to take actions through your site. It defines tools, resources, and prompts that an AI can call — essentially an API designed for machines to invoke, not humans to click.

llms.txt is a convention for telling AI agents what your site is about, what's on it, and how to read it. It's a structured document — usually a single file at /llms.txt — that summarizes your site's content, purpose, and key URLs in a format optimized for LLM consumption rather than browser rendering.

The real question isn't which standard to choose. It's: what does your site need AI agents to do? Read it, or act through it?

What llms.txt Actually Does

When a language model needs to answer a question about your product, it has two options: scrape your HTML and parse it (expensive, lossy, slow), or read a clean summary you've prepared for it. llms.txt is that summary.

A well-written llms.txt tells an AI agent:

  • What your product or company does, in plain language
  • Which pages matter and what each one is for
  • What terminology you use (so the model doesn't hallucinate alternatives)
  • What your pricing, features, and integrations are
  • Where to find your documentation, API reference, and changelog

The practical effect: when someone asks Claude or ChatGPT about your product category, the model has a clean, authoritative source to cite rather than a patchwork of scraped fragments. You're not just hoping the AI picks up the right information — you're handing it directly.

CEO tip: Think of llms.txt as the cover letter you write for AI models. Your actual HTML is the full document — but this is the first thing the model reads, and it shapes everything that follows.

What MCP Actually Does

MCP is different in kind, not just degree. Where llms.txt is about being understood, MCP is about being used.

An MCP server exposes a set of typed tools — functions an AI agent can call with specific inputs and receive structured outputs from. If an agent is helping a user book a meeting, it might call your list_availability tool, then create_booking, then send_confirmation. All without a human navigating your UI.

MCP is Anthropic's open standard, but other AI providers are converging on compatible patterns. The key characteristics:

  • Tools: Functions the AI can invoke (search, create, update, delete)
  • Resources: Data the AI can read (documents, records, live state)
  • Prompts: Templated interactions the AI can use as starting points
  • Sampling: The ability for servers to request completions back from the model

If your site is a platform — something users do things through — MCP is what lets AI agents do those things autonomously.

Who Needs What

Start with llms.txt if:

  • Your primary goal is getting cited in AI-generated answers
  • You run a content site, documentation hub, or marketing site
  • You want quick wins with low implementation cost
  • You don't have engineering resources to spare right now
  • You're still figuring out your agent strategy

Start with MCP if:

  • You're building a platform that agents will use to take actions
  • Your API is already well-defined and stable
  • You want to integrate into AI agent workflows (Claude, Cursor plugins, etc.)
  • Your users are developers or AI-forward teams
  • Agentic automation is a core part of your product roadmap

You probably need both if:

  • You run a SaaS product with a content/docs layer
  • You want both discoverability (llms.txt) and utility (MCP)
  • Your site serves both end-users and developers building on top of your platform

The sequencing rule: llms.txt first, MCP second. llms.txt costs almost nothing and immediately improves how AI models describe your product. MCP requires real engineering investment — do it when you have a clear agentic use case, not just because it sounds forward-looking.

Common Mistakes

Treating llms.txt as a one-time task

A stale llms.txt is almost worse than no llms.txt — it gives AI models confidently wrong information about your product. Treat it like your docs: update it when features ship, when pricing changes, when you add integrations.

Building MCP before having a product that needs it

MCP is powerful but expensive to maintain. If you don't have agents actively using your tools, you're paying engineering cost for zero user benefit. Build it when there's a concrete use case — not as infrastructure speculation.

Writing llms.txt for humans

Your llms.txt should be dense with signal and light on prose. No marketing language, no metaphors, no vague value props. Use short declarative sentences: what you are, what you do, what your key features are, what your pricing is, where to find your API. The model doesn't need to be convinced — it just needs to be informed.

Exposing sensitive data through MCP tools

MCP tools run with whatever permissions you grant them. Scope them tightly. An agent that can read everything can also be prompted to exfiltrate everything. Apply the same principle of least privilege you'd apply to any API key.

The CEO Take

llms.txt and MCP are both part of the broader shift toward agent-first optimization — the idea that AI agents are now a primary audience for your site, not a secondary one.

If you've been putting off both because the landscape felt too unsettled, the practical answer is: implement llms.txt this week (it takes a few hours), and add MCP to your roadmap when you have a concrete agentic use case to drive it.

The sites that get found, cited, and used by AI agents in 2026 are the ones that made themselves readable and actionable — not the ones that waited for the standards to finalize.
