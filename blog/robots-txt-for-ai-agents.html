robots.txt for AI Agents: Why Your Current File May Be Blocking the Wrong Crawlers

Most robots.txt files were written to manage search engine crawlers. AI agents operate differently — and without an update, you may be blocking the agents you want to reach your content while doing nothing to stop the scrapers you don't.

The robots.txt Assumption Gap

When robots.txt was designed in 1994, the threat model was straightforward: Googlebot and its peers needed guidance on which pages to index, and site owners needed a way to exclude private sections from search results. The protocol worked because major search engines chose to honor it — not because there was any technical enforcement mechanism. robots.txt has always been a convention, not a security boundary.

That convention held reasonably well for three decades because the major crawlers were operated by companies with reputational stakes in being good citizens. Googlebot, Bingbot, and Slurp were identifiable, their user agents were stable, and they generally followed Disallow directives reliably.

AI agents have complicated this picture in two distinct ways. First, the major AI labs launched their own crawlers to train models and retrieve context — GPTBot (OpenAI), ClaudeBot (Anthropic), CCBot (Common Crawl, which feeds several models), PerplexityBot, and others. These crawlers introduced a new category of traffic that most existing robots.txt files hadn't accounted for. Second, a long tail of low-quality scrapers began using AI-related user agents — some legitimate, many not — making it harder to reason about what any given robots.txt configuration actually allows.

The practical result: many sites are running robots.txt configurations that were never designed for this environment and are producing effects their owners didn't intend.

The Major AI Crawlers and Their User Agents

Before editing your robots.txt, it helps to know who you're dealing with. Here are the primary AI-affiliated crawlers active in 2026:

User Agent Operator Purpose Honors robots.txt?
GPTBot OpenAI Training data + ChatGPT retrieval Yes (documented)
ClaudeBot Anthropic Training data + Claude retrieval Yes (documented)
CCBot Common Crawl Open dataset used by many models Yes (documented)
PerplexityBot Perplexity AI Real-time search retrieval Yes (documented)
Applebot-Extended Apple Apple Intelligence training Yes (documented)
OAI-SearchBot OpenAI ChatGPT search feature Yes (documented)

The key observation: the legitimate AI crawlers from major labs all document their user agents and claim to honor robots.txt. This is genuinely useful — it means you can selectively allow or block them. The crawlers that don't honor robots.txt are, by definition, operating outside the convention entirely, and no robots.txt configuration will stop them.

Implication: robots.txt controls the crawlers that respect it. If your goal is to stop malicious scraping, robots.txt is the wrong tool. Use rate limiting, CAPTCHAs, and WAF rules. robots.txt is for managing relationships with good-faith crawlers — and that's actually a meaningful power once you understand it.

The Three Most Common Misconfiguration Patterns

1. Blanket Disallow via wildcard User-Agent

Some sites block all non-Googlebot traffic with a configuration like:

# Blocks everything except Googlebot
User-agent: *
Disallow: /

User-agent: Googlebot
Allow: /

This configuration was designed to protect private staging sites or content-heavy pages from being indexed by secondary crawlers. But it also blocks GPTBot, ClaudeBot, and PerplexityBot — meaning your content won't be retrieved for AI citation, won't appear in Perplexity answers, and won't be included in training data you might actually want to participate in.

If this configuration is on your production site intentionally, that's a valid choice. But many sites have inherited it from a developer who was trying to prevent duplicate indexing of a staging environment, and it ended up in production by mistake.

2. Blocking AI crawlers you want, allowing ones you don't care about

Some site owners read early coverage of AI training data concerns and added blanket AI crawler blocks without distinguishing between crawlers they have reasons to block (training data scrapers) and crawlers they want (real-time retrieval agents like PerplexityBot and OAI-SearchBot).

The distinction matters. Blocking CCBot and Amazonbot — both primarily used for training data aggregation — is a legitimate choice for sites that don't want to contribute to model training. Blocking PerplexityBot means your content won't appear in Perplexity's answers. Blocking OAI-SearchBot means ChatGPT won't retrieve your pages when users ask about topics you cover. These are different outcomes with different business implications.

3. No AI-specific entries at all

The default robots.txt on most sites was written before AI crawlers existed and has never been updated. These files typically manage Googlebot, Bingbot, and maybe a few spam crawlers — and they implicitly allow all unspecified user agents via the User-agent: * wildcard with permissive rules.

This isn't necessarily wrong, but it means you've made no active choices about AI crawlers. You're getting whatever the default behavior is for each crawler, which may or may not align with your goals.

A Practical robots.txt Template for the Agent Era

Here's a template that makes explicit, intentional choices about AI crawlers. Adjust based on your actual goals.

# Standard search engines — allow everything
User-agent: Googlebot
Allow: /

User-agent: Bingbot
Allow: /

# AI retrieval agents — allow (these cite your content in answers)
User-agent: GPTBot
Allow: /

User-agent: OAI-SearchBot
Allow: /

User-agent: ClaudeBot
Allow: /

User-agent: PerplexityBot
Allow: /

# Training data aggregators — block if you prefer not to contribute
# Remove these blocks if you want to participate in training datasets
User-agent: CCBot
Disallow: /

User-agent: Amazonbot
Disallow: /

User-agent: Applebot-Extended
Disallow: /

# Catch-all for unrecognized crawlers
User-agent: *
Allow: /

# Always block these paths regardless of crawler
User-agent: *
Disallow: /admin/
Disallow: /api/private/
Disallow: /internal/

Note on Applebot-Extended: The standard Applebot handles Safari search suggestions and general Apple indexing. Applebot-Extended is the extended version used specifically for Apple Intelligence training. You can block the extended version while allowing standard Apple indexing by specifying both directives separately.

robots.txt and llms.txt: Complementary, Not Competing

If you've already implemented llms.txt, your robots.txt configuration should align with it. A site that blocks GPTBot in robots.txt but maintains a detailed llms.txt is sending contradictory signals — the llms.txt is written to help AI agents understand your site, but the robots.txt tells GPTBot not to visit.

The coherent pairing: allow the retrieval agents you want citing your content in robots.txt, and give those agents the clearest possible picture of what to cite via llms.txt. The combination is what gets you reliably included in AI-generated answers.

How to Audit Your Current Configuration

Run through this checklist before making changes:

  1. Read your current robots.txt: curl https://yourdomain.com/robots.txt
  2. Check for wildcard blocks: Does User-agent: * / Disallow: / appear anywhere? If so, are you intentionally blocking all non-whitelisted crawlers?
  3. Identify AI crawler entries: Are GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, OAI-SearchBot explicitly mentioned? If not, they're governed by your wildcard rule — intentionally or not.
  4. Verify your goals: Do you want AI agents citing your content? Do you want to participate in training datasets? Answer these explicitly before writing rules.
  5. Check Disallow paths: Are there paths that should be excluded from AI crawlers for data sensitivity reasons? Add them explicitly rather than relying on inherited rules.
